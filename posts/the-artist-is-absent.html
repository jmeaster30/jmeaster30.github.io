Sometimes I think about communication

As humans, we communicate all the time. The most obvious form of communication is verbal communication. Most people talk with others every single day, where it be with your boss, with your spouse, with your cats.

These forms of communication fit the Shannon Weaver model of communication pretty well. In the Shannon Weaver model, there is an information source (Alice's brain) that encodes (turns thoughts into language) some message (what Alice wants to say) and transmits (speaks) that message through a channel (the air). Then that message gets decoded (Bob's ears turn sounds into thoughts) and then the destination (Bob's brain) receives the final message. When Bob responds to what Alice said, that is the feedback and now you have communication. Something else that is in this diagram is noise. This noise is depicted as only happening in the channel but it can happen in the encoder, channel, or the decoder steps as well (probably can happen in the sender and receiver steps too adding that idea into my thoughts doesn't change what I am about to say). This noise can come as a horn blaring from a car that is driving by or the letter you wrote got rained on and some of the ink smudged or interferrance in radio waves. Noise can also come from errors in the encoding and decoding process. If we think of compression algorithms as a communication channel (they have encoders and decoders and the result is only meaningful to a human before the encoding and after the decoding), if the encoder is written perfectly and the decoder clears the code table one iteration early, the receiver can't fully understand the result and the noise of this off-by-one error has completely disrupted the message. A similar issue can arise if Alice and Bob are having a riveting discussion about trees. Alice has only ever lived in Wisconsin around pine trees and oak tree (and she doesn't know much about any other trees). When Bob begins to describe a tree as a long single trunk that droops with long large branches that only come out of the very top of the tree and on those branches are a bunch of really long thin leaves, Alice will be pretty confused. She has never seen a tree like that, so the image of a palm tree can't form completely.

Sometimes I think about stories

In highschool, my least favorite subject was English class. I never understood why it was necessary to nitpick every detail of a book to pull understanding out of it. Why do we need to know exactly what the author is trying to say with this story? Was the pond in The Catcher in the Rye really a symbol of Holden's reluctance to transition from childhood to adulthood? Or was it just that ponds and parks are calm places that people use to clear their mind and exist in nature? Do any of these books really have any meaning?

Of course these books have meaning. They have to. Their existance requires meaning. If you had nothing to say then why would you try to communicate it through a story? Authors attempt to communicate some message or messages by writing these stories onto paper such that someone or even themselves, can come back at a later time and experience the story and gain some message. The art of story-telling is interesting because you are using a variety of languages (written in books, visual in paintings, aural in music, combinations of several in movies and video games) to build up this message in another person's mind but the person isn't there with the artist as the artist is making it. This creates a broken model of communication where there isn't any feedback. It is all interpretation by the receiver. When people are consuming the art, the artist is absent.

Sometimes I think about artists

10:47am I realize these questions are ultimately unimportant. I could use git blame to see who did it but the only thing that matters right now is that I fix this bug so we can get a patch released. The fix was simple so I decided to spend a little bit of time and make the surrounding code better.

11:05am The pull request is up, it gets approvals, QA verifies the fix, and we deploy that night to production.

The rest of the day I think about The Catcher in the Rye. The artist took their interpretation of the requirements and encoded those thoughts into logic and then into a programming language. The compiler takes that code and encodes it into machine code for the computer to decode and do the work it needs to. Then when I received that bug to work on, I decoded the programming language into my own understanding of the current logic and compared that logic with the logic I encoded from the requirements. This is a massive communcation network with possibilities for noise to warp the overall message. Some areas we sacrifice that control for the purpose of time and productivity. Other areas we have a lot of control over. If the developer who created the code is no longer available because they got hit by a bus or won a million dollars, the artist is absent. If the developer who created the code is still available, you can communicate with them directly! Or can you? Software projects are constantly changing and as we develop these projects our experience has changed. There were some of those times where I couldn't hold back and I found I was the one who created the horrible code that caused loads of problems and headaches. Even if the same human is around, those artists too are absent.

If the artist is absent, the only meaning that really matters is the meaning the artists that are not make. You can analyze the systemic issues that could possibly make someone make those decisions but any analysis on the past is warped by our new knowledge we've gained in the mean time.

Sometimes I think about mistakes and getting better

It is impossible to reflect on the past without it becoming warped with your new knowledge. I watched a conference talk about Three-Mile Island and how many times the engineers on call screwed up massively and how obvious these mistakes were. Then, after the presenter went through everything the engineers did wrong, they talked about how the report on the incident didn't take an approach of blaming and took an approach of understanding. The investigators put themselves in the shoes of the engineers. Instead of asking "What the hell were these folks thinking?", they asked "What lead them to believe they were doing the right thing?". This reframing of the question lead to loads of improvements to other nuclear reactors. They took something bad and worked on improving.

When I find issues in code at work or code in my personal projects, I find I am constantly in this state where I can't talk to the person who wrote the code in the first place. Even comments in the code are affected by this noise and get stale. All I have is what I know now and the problem that needs fixing and the goal of improving.
